Network of Networks

The internet isn't one big network — it's thousands of networks that agree to cooperate. Here's the hierarchy.

What is a Local Network?

A local network (LAN) is a group of devices that can communicate directly with each other — no router needed, just switches.

  • A device knows something is local by checking the first X bits of its IP address against its own — if they match, it's on the same network
  • Within the LAN, switches forward traffic using MAC addresses
  • The value of X is set by the router (this is the subnet mask)

The Players

End Systems / Hosts

The devices users interact with — laptops, phones, servers. They run application programs and sit at the "edges" of the internet.

Switches

Forward packets within a single local network using MAC addresses. They operate at the Link Layer.

Routers

Connect different networks. They forward packets based on IP addresses and operate at the Network Layer.

ISPs

Internet Service Providers are themselves networks of routers and links. Small ISPs connect to larger ones, up to backbone networks.

Why this hierarchy?

No single organization can own or manage the entire internet. Instead, each ISP manages its own network and buys "transit" from larger ISPs to reach the rest of the internet. This creates a market-based, decentralized design that scales globally. Top-tier (Tier-1) ISPs peer directly with each other for free, carrying backbone traffic over high-speed fiber.

Protocols Are the Glue

Different networks can run different hardware — fiber, copper, WiFi, satellite. They cooperate because they all speak the same protocols: TCP, IP, HTTP, SMTP, etc.

A protocol is a set of rules defining:

  • Message format — what fields exist and in what order
  • Message order — who speaks first, who responds
  • What actions to take on receipt
  • How errors are handled
Why Protocols?

Without protocols, a packet from your MacBook might be completely misunderstood by a server running different software. Protocols are like agreeing to speak English at an international conference — the content varies, but the language is shared.
