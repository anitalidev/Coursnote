Circuit vs Packet Switching

The internet was built on a deliberate choice: break messages into packets instead of reserving dedicated lines. Here's why.

Circuit Switching — The Telephone Approach

The original telephone network used circuit switching. When you called someone, a physical path was reserved entirely for your call — from start to finish — for the whole duration.

Multiplexing Circuit Lines

Since reserving an entire physical wire per call wastes most of its capacity, two methods split one wire among many calls:

Frequency Division (FDM)

Divides the bandwidth into frequency bands — like radio stations each on their own frequency. Each call gets its slice of spectrum simultaneously.

Time Division (TDM)

Divides time into repeating slots — each call gets the full bandwidth, but only during its time slot. Like taking turns very fast.

Circuit Switching: Advantages & Disadvantages

✓ Advantages

  • Guaranteed, constant rate
  • Deterministic — every bit takes the same path
  • No queuing delay once connected
  • Great for continuous streams (voice calls)

✗ Disadvantages

  • Reserved line sits idle during silence
  • Cannot share line with others
  • Bursty traffic = wasted capacity
  • Physical break = connection lost

Packet Switching — The Internet Way

Circuit switching is good for things like phone calls, since it is generally expected for a phone call to involve continuous speaking, so the reserved line is consistently used to send data. Thus, in modern times circuit switching still has some use cases. However, for other cases like web browsing, file transfers, and most internet traffic, usage is bursty — you request a page, get a burst, then nothing while you read. Reserving a dedicated line for that is wasteful.

Why Packets?

Instead of reserving a whole lane on a highway for one car, packet switching is more like a shared road: many cars (packets) share the road and route independently. The tradeoff: you can't guarantee arrival time, and if there's a traffic jam, some cars might be turned away (dropped packets). As well, different cars may have to take different routes to try and get to their destination, even if they have the same destination.

In packet switching:

  • Messages are broken into packets containing some data + metadata (destination, ordering for when one message is broken into multiple packets, etc.)
  • Each packet is sent separately and may take different paths
  • Routers use forwarding tables (keyed by IP address) to decide where each packet goes next
  • Each link carries one packet at a time — others must wait in a queue
  • If the queue is full, packets are dropped and lost permanently

✓ Advantages

  • No wasted reservation — links only used when data is actually sent
  • Many users can share the same link efficiently
  • No setup delay before sending
  • Resilient — packets can reroute around failures
  • Works well for bursty traffic (web, email, files)

✗ Disadvantages

  • No guaranteed delivery time — variable delay
  • Packets may arrive out of order and must be reassembled
  • Full queues cause permanent packet loss
  • Unpredictable jitter — bad for real-time audio/video
  • Overhead from per-packet headers (metadata)
