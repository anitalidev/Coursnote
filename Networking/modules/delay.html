Delay & Packet Loss

Every packet passes through four types of delay at each node. Understanding each one tells you where to optimize.

The Four Sources of Nodal Delay

When a packet arrives at a router, it goes through four stages — in order. Since they happen sequentially, the delays add up.

1 · Processing Delay

Time to read the packet header and decide where to send it next.

Characteristics: Small, roughly constant. Usually microseconds.

2 · Queuing Delay

Time waiting in various switch/routers' queues for the link to be free. Depends entirely on how congested the network is.

Characteristics: Highly variable. Zero when empty; can be very large when congested. If the queue fills up — packets are dropped.

3 · Transmission Delay

Time to push all bits of the packet onto the link.

L / R

L = packet size in bits  |  R = link bandwidth in bps

4 · Propagation Delay

Time for a bit to physically travel across the link medium (wire, fiber, air).

D / V

D = distance of link  |  V = propagation speed (~2×10⁸ m/s in fiber)

The total end-to-end delay across a path of N routers is the sum of all four delay types at every hop: dend-to-end = N × (dproc + dqueue + dtrans + dprop). Each router adds its own set — so a packet crossing 10 routers accumulates 10 layers of nodal delay.

Transmission vs Propagation — Easy to Confuse!

Transmission delay is about the size of your packet and the speed of your link — like how long it takes to load a truck. Propagation delay is about distance — like how long the truck takes to drive. A huge packet on a slow link has high transmission delay. A small packet crossing a continent has high propagation delay.

What the animation reveals

Queuing delay is the most variable. It depends entirely on how busy the router is — with an empty queue it's zero, but under heavy load it can dominate all other delays combined. This is why the same route can feel fast at 2 am and sluggish at peak hours.

Processing delay is essentially fixed. Inspecting a packet header and looking up the forwarding table takes roughly the same time regardless of network conditions — modern routers do this in nanoseconds using dedicated hardware.

Transmission delay scales with packet size. Pushing bits onto the wire takes longer for bigger packets and slower links (L/R). Fragmentation and smaller MTUs exist partly to keep this delay bounded.

Propagation delay is also fixed for a given path. Once bits are on the wire, they travel at a constant fraction of the speed of light — nothing in software can change this. It's purely physics and distance, which is why satellite links (≥600 ms round-trip) feel laggy no matter how much bandwidth you add.

Queue Traffic Intensity

The key metric for queuing delay is traffic intensity:

Traffic Intensity = (a × L) / R

Where a = avg packets/second arriving, L = avg packet size (bits), R = link bandwidth (bps).

  • If intensity > 1 → queue grows forever → system breaks down. Never design for this.
  • As intensity → 1 → queuing delay grows rapidly (non-linear!)
  • Below ~0.7 → queuing delay stays manageable

Packet Loss

Every router has a finite queue. When the queue is full and a new packet arrives, that packet is dropped and permanently lost. There's no recovery at the network layer — it's gone.

  • Packet loss at the IP layer requires higher layers (like TCP) to detect and retransmit
  • UDP does not retransmit — lost packets are gone forever
  • High traffic intensity → frequent loss → worse goodput even if throughput looks OK
Why Packets Are Just Dropped (Not Delayed)

The internet uses "drop-tail" queuing: when the buffer is full, new arrivals are dropped. The alternative — storing them somewhere else — would require unbounded memory. Dropping is a deliberate design choice that signals congestion to higher layers, letting TCP slow down. This is the internet's congestion control mechanism.
