Performance Metrics

Before you can make the internet faster, you need to measure it precisely. These six metrics are how networking engineers think about performance.

Bandwidth

The maximum rate data can flow over a link. Think of it as the size of a pipe. Does not describe how much is actually flowing — just the capacity.

Bandwidth (bps) = max bits per second

Note: Kb = 2¹⁰ bits (binary, used for storage), but Kbps = 10³ bps (decimal, used for link speeds). This trips people up!

Throughput

The actual rate data moves end-to-end. Limited by the slowest link in the path — the bottleneck.

Throughput = min(link₁, link₂, ..., linkₙ)

Even if your home connection is 1 Gbps, if the server's link is 10 Mbps, your throughput is 10 Mbps.

Goodput

The rate at which useful application data arrives. Excludes headers, retransmissions, and overhead.

Goodput ≤ Throughput ≤ Bandwidth. If you retransmit lots of lost packets, throughput can be high but goodput low.

Latency

Time from when something is sent until it is received. The "something" must be defined consistently — a whole packet, a single bit, etc.

Round Trip Time

Latency to and back.

Latency is directional (one-way). RTT measures the round trip.

Bandwidth, Throughput & Goodput — The Pipe Analogy

The relationship between these three metrics is easiest to understand with a pipe: bandwidth is the pipe's diameter (max capacity), throughput is all the water actually flowing (including headers and retransmits), and goodput is only the useful water that reaches the application.

The ×8 trap

Your ISP advertises 100 Mbps. You download a file and your browser shows 12.5 MB/s. Is something wrong? No — 100 Mbps ÷ 8 = 12.5 MB/s. Link speeds are always in bits; file sizes and transfer speeds shown by apps are in bytes. Always divide by 8 when crossing between them.

Worked Example — Throughput vs Goodput

Given a scenario, here is how to calculate each metric step by step.

Given
Bandwidth
100 Mbps
Total sent
800 MB in 100 s
Retransmissions
100 MB
Step 1 — Throughput
Throughput = Total bytes sent ÷ Time
           = 800 MB ÷ 100 s
           = 8 MB/s
           = 8 × 8 = 64 Mbps (64% of 100 Mbps bandwidth)
Step 2 — Goodput
Goodput = (Total sent − Retransmissions) ÷ Time
        = (800 MB − 100 MB) ÷ 100 s
        = 700 MB ÷ 100 s
        = 7 MB/s
        = 7 × 8 = 56 Mbps (56% of 100 Mbps bandwidth)

Round-Trip Time (RTT)

RTT is the total time for something to travel to a destination and for the response to come back. The ping command measures RTT.

RTT = send latency + receive latency

Jitter

Jitter is the variation in latency over time. If packets sometimes take 10ms and sometimes 100ms, that's high jitter.

Causes of jitter:

  • Different paths — packets may take different routes with different delays
  • Network congestion — queues fill up unpredictably
  • No packet prioritization — all traffic treated equally
  • Wireless interference — WiFi signal varies with environment
  • Old/slow hardware — processing takes inconsistent time
Why Jitter Matters

For a file download, jitter doesn't matter — you just want it to eventually arrive. But for a video call, if your voice packets arrive at wildly different times, they can't be played back smoothly. Real-time applications are jitter-sensitive; batch applications are not. This is one reason VoIP and video conferencing are harder to design than HTTP.
